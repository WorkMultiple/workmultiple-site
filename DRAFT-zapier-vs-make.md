# Zapier vs Make for Small Business Automation: Which Tool Wins in 2026?

If you're running a small business and drowning in manual tasks, you've probably heard of Zapier or Make. Both platforms promise to automate the work that buries your day: syncing data between apps, sending notifications when something changes, updating spreadsheets automatically, managing leads, and handling customer follow-ups.

The difference matters. Pick the wrong one, and you'll either hit a wall with limitations that force you to rebuild everything, or you'll overpay for capabilities you don't need. We tested both platforms to see which one actually delivers for small business owners who want results without the complexity.

## What Problem Are We Solving?

Small businesses typically need automation for repeatable, high-friction processes. Examples: When a customer fills out a form, add them to your CRM and send a welcome email. When a new lead lands in Slack, create a task in your project manager. When a spreadsheet updates, trigger a follow-up workflow. Process data across multiple apps without manual copying.

Both Zapier and Make solve this exact problem. But they approach it differently.

## Zapier: The Easier Path

Zapier is the established player. It's been around since 2011, powers over 2 million active workflows, and integrates with more apps than any competitor: 8,000+ integrations across CRM, email, project management, spreadsheets, and e-commerce tools.

### What Makes Zapier Simple

Zapier guides you step-by-step. You choose a trigger (something that starts your workflow), then add actions (things that happen next). The interface is linear and intuitive. Most small business owners can build their first working Zap in under 10 minutes without reading documentation.

The platform also includes templates for common workflows. Need to add new HubSpot contacts to a Google spreadsheet? Zapier has a pre-built template you just turn on. This matters. Templates compress days of setup into minutes.

Zapier's newest feature is Copilot, which lets you describe what you want in plain language: "When someone fills out my contact form, add them to my CRM and send them an email." Zapier builds the automation for you.

### Zapier Pricing

Zapier charges based on tasks (actions). One task = one action executed. The structure is straightforward.

**Free plan:** 100 tasks/month. You can use up to two-step Zaps (trigger + one action). Free forever, but extremely limiting.

**Professional plan:** $19.99/month gets you multi-step Zaps, webhooks for advanced integrations, and much higher task allowance (varies, but roughly 750+ tasks/month depending on overage status).

**Team plan:** $69/month for 25 users, shared Zaps, and priority support.

**Enterprise:** Custom pricing for large organizations.

The issue: As your automation needs grow, task usage climbs fast. Running 20 daily automations that each count 5 tasks will consume 2,700 tasks per month. At that point, you're approaching $50-75/month or higher. Scale further, and Zapier becomes expensive.

### Zapier Strengths

- **Easiest learning curve.** Non-technical people can build automations immediately.
- **Largest integration library.** 8,000+ apps means you'll find what you need.
- **Pre-built templates.** Get started faster with existing workflows for common tasks.
- **Stability and reliability.** The platform is mature, updated frequently, and rarely breaks.
- **Natural language workflow builder.** Zapier Copilot lets you describe automations in plain English.

### Zapier Weaknesses

- **Limited workflow complexity.** Maximum 100 steps per Zap. Complex logic with multiple branches requires workarounds.
- **Costly at scale.** Task-based pricing makes high-volume automation expensive. Businesses running thousands of tasks monthly can see bills exceed $100-200.
- **Weak error handling.** Limited options for handling failures. Workflows often stall if a single step breaks.
- **No data loop tools.** Can't iterate over arrays or process lists without building custom logic through workarounds.
- **Rigid structure.** Workflows must be linear. Conditional branches exist, but the interface doesn't visually show complex logic as clearly as competitors.

## Make: The Flexible Alternative

Make (formerly Integromat) is the flexible workhorse. It supports 2,800+ integrations but excels at complex workflows. Where Zapier forces you into simple linear paths, Make lets you branch, loop, filter, and retry with visual clarity.

### What Makes Make Powerful

Make's visual builder shows your entire workflow on screen. You see branches, conditions, error handlers, and loops all at once. If something goes wrong, you can see exactly where it failed and fix it.

Make doesn't limit workflow steps. You can build scenarios with dozens of modules, parallel processing, conditional routing, and data transformation. Data handling is superior: Make can iterate over arrays, format JSON, parse complex objects, and apply filters without hacks.

### Make Pricing

Make uses a credit-based model. One operation equals roughly one credit (though this varies by complexity). It's less intuitive than Zapier's task model, but often cheaper at scale.

**Free plan:** 1,000 credits/month. Basic builder, limited integrations.

**Basic plan:** $9.99/month (or approximately that range) for 10,000 credits/month.

**Pro plan:** Starts around $30-50/month with 100,000 credits/month.

**Team plan:** For collaborative workflows and team permissions.

**Enterprise:** Custom pricing.

The advantage: 10,000 credits is roughly equivalent to 100-200 tasks in Zapier (depending on workflow complexity). At that volume, Make costs about 1/3 the price. For small businesses running complex automations, the savings are significant.

### Make Strengths

- **Cost-effective at scale.** Operations pricing is 3-5x cheaper than Zapier for high-volume automation.
- **Handles complex workflows.** Unlimited steps, branching, looping, and conditional logic.
- **Better error handling.** Detailed error handlers and retry logic prevent workflows from breaking silently.
- **Superior data tools.** Iterate over arrays, format data, parse JSON, and apply complex filters without workarounds.
- **Parallel processing.** Run multiple steps simultaneously, not sequentially.
- **Custom integrations.** HTTP module lets you connect directly to any service's data layer, even if Make doesn't have a pre-built integration.

### Make Weaknesses

- **Steeper learning curve.** Non-technical users need a week or two to get comfortable. The blank canvas can feel overwhelming at first.
- **Fewer pre-built templates.** Zapier has more turnkey solutions. With Make, you'll build more from scratch.
- **Smaller integration library.** 2,800 apps is solid but not 8,000+. For niche tools, you may need to build custom connections through Make's data layer module.
- **Polling counts.** Triggers check for updates every 5 minutes by default. Each check counts as operations. For high-frequency triggers, costs add up.
- **Less polish in UX.** Zapier feels more refined. Make works better but looks less polished.

## Choose Zapier If...

- You're new to automation and want a quick learning curve.
- Your workflows are simple and linear (trigger + 2-5 actions).
- You need to integrate with mainstream apps and want pre-built templates.
- Your team includes non-technical employees who'll set up automations themselves.
- You prioritize stability and polish over flexibility.
- Your task volume stays under 5,000 per month.

## Choose Make If...

- You need complex workflows with conditional logic and multiple branches.
- You're already comfortable with no-code tools or have some technical background.
- Your automation volume exceeds 5,000 tasks per month (cost becomes a factor).
- You want detailed error handling and the ability to see your entire workflow at once.
- You need to iterate over lists, format JSON, or handle complex data structures.
- You're willing to spend a week learning a more powerful tool to save money and build better workflows.

## The Verdict

**For small businesses starting out:** Zapier wins. If you're automating your first few processes and don't have the time or patience to learn complex tools, Zapier's simplicity is worth the slightly higher cost. You'll get automations running in hours, not days.

**For small businesses with recurring automation needs:** Make wins. If you're running consistent automations monthly, Make's pricing becomes significantly cheaper. At 10,000 operations per month, you'll save $50-100 per month compared to Zapier. Over a year, that's real money. Plus, you'll build better workflows with Make's conditional logic and error handling.

**For growing teams:** Make has the edge. As your business scales and automation becomes central to operations, Make's flexibility means you won't outgrow it. Zapier's 100-step limit will eventually force you to rebuild workflows or add intermediate tools.

Neither tool is objectively better. Zapier prioritizes simplicity; Make prioritizes power. Your choice depends on whether you value getting started quickly (Zapier) or building efficiently at scale (Make).

### Next Steps

Start with both. Both platforms offer free trials. Build one simple automation in each and see which interface feels natural to you. Most small businesses find their answer in 30 minutes of hands-on testing.

If you go with Zapier, start with a template. If you go with Make, spend time with their documentation and watch a setup tutorial. The learning investment in Make pays back faster than you might expect.
