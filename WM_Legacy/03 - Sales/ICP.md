# WorkMultiple - Ideal Customer Profile

> **This document defines who we sell to.** Every lead list, scoring rubric, outreach message, and sales conversation should be checked against this profile. If a prospect doesn't match, move on.

---

## Summary

WorkMultiple's ideal customer is a high-ticket specialized contractor (custom home builder, high-end remodeler, or commercial HVAC company) with 2 to 25 employees, doing $1M to $15M in revenue. They have complex, repeatable workflows that consume hours every week: lead follow-up, client communication during long projects, subcontractor coordination, and post-project follow-up. They know their operations are inefficient but don't have the tech staff or know-how to fix it. They want someone to come in, build the solution, and keep it running.

---

## Firmographics

### Company Size
- **Employees:** 2 to 25
- **Sweet spot:** 5 to 15 employees. Large enough to have a dedicated office manager or project coordinator who is overwhelmed with manual work. Small enough that they cannot hire dedicated ops or tech staff.

### Revenue Range
- **$1M to $15M annually**
- They have budget to invest in efficiency. At $1M+ in revenue, a $4,500 engagement is a fraction of a single project's value. They think in terms of ROI: "If this saves me one lost lead per quarter, it pays for itself."

### Geography
- **Primary:** United States
- **Secondary:** Canada (English-speaking markets with similar contractor business structures)
- Metro and suburban areas with active construction markets are strongest. Areas with housing booms, renovation demand, or commercial development are ideal.

### Industry Focus: High-Ticket Specialized Contractors

**1. Custom Home Builders**

Why they fit: Custom homes are $300K-$2M+ projects running 6-18 months. The coordination complexity is enormous: 15-25 subcontractors, hundreds of client decisions, permitting, inspections, change orders, draw schedules. A 10-person custom builder might have one project coordinator managing all of this manually. The workflows are long, repeatable across projects, and ripe for automation.

Common pain points:
- Leads going cold because the team is too busy managing active projects to follow up on new inquiries
- Clients feeling uninformed during long build timelines (they go weeks without hearing from the builder)
- Subcontractor scheduling that requires constant phone calls and texts
- Change orders documented on napkins or not at all, leading to margin erosion
- No systematic post-completion follow-up (warranty, reviews, referrals)

Tools they typically use: Buildertrend, CoConstruct, or spreadsheets. Some use Procore. Most still rely heavily on phone calls, texts, and email for day-to-day coordination.

**2. High-End Remodelers (Kitchen, Bath, Whole-Home)**

Why they fit: High-end remodels ($50K-$500K per project) involve 2-6 month timelines with intensive client communication. The client expectation is high because they're spending significant money and living in or near an active job site. Remodelers need to manage design consultations, material selections, subcontractor schedules, and client approvals. Small teams (5-15 people) handle multiple concurrent projects.

Common pain points:
- Estimate and proposal turnaround is slow, costing them bids
- Clients want weekly updates but the team doesn't have time to write them
- Material selections and change orders create confusion between what was agreed and what gets built
- Post-project review and referral requests don't happen consistently
- The owner is spending 10+ hours/week on admin instead of on-site or selling new projects

Tools they typically use: Buildertrend, Houzz Pro, or basic tools (QuickBooks + spreadsheets). Some use JobTread or AccuLynx.

**3. Commercial HVAC Companies**

Why they fit: Commercial HVAC involves service agreements, preventive maintenance schedules, and project-based installations that create complex operational workflows. A 15-person commercial HVAC company might manage 200+ service agreements with recurring maintenance visits, plus project bids, installations, and warranty work. The coordination between technicians, clients, suppliers, and office staff is constant.

Common pain points:
- Service agreement renewals falling through the cracks (revenue left on the table)
- Bid and proposal generation is manual and slow
- Dispatching and technician communication is handled through calls and texts
- Client communication about scheduled maintenance is inconsistent
- Post-installation follow-up (warranty registration, satisfaction survey, referral request) doesn't happen

Tools they typically use: ServiceTitan, Housecall Pro, or basic scheduling tools. Some use FieldEdge or Service Fusion. Most handle communication and follow-up manually.

## Psychographics

### Who Makes the Decision

The buyer depends on company size:

- **At companies with 2-10 people:** The **owner or founder**. They're hands-on in operations, feel the pain daily, and make purchasing decisions quickly. They're the person who lost a $75K lead because nobody followed up for three days.

- **At companies with 10-25 people:** The **office manager, project coordinator, or operations lead**. The person actually doing the manual work. They champion the solution internally because it directly reduces their workload. The owner signs off, but this person drives the decision.

### Pain Points (What They'd Actually Say)

1. "I lost a $75K remodel because we took three days to call back. They went with someone faster."
2. "My project coordinator spends half her day on the phone with subs. She doesn't have time to actually coordinate projects."
3. "Clients call asking for updates and we don't have a good answer because nobody wrote down where things stand."
4. "I know I should be asking for reviews and referrals after every project, but it just doesn't happen."
5. "I'm the owner and I'm still the one sending estimates at 10pm because we don't have a system."
6. "Change orders are killing our margins. Half of them never get documented properly."
7. "We use Buildertrend for scheduling but everything else is email, texts, and Post-its."

### Buying Triggers (What Makes Them Ready to Act Now)

- They just lost a significant lead to a competitor who responded faster
- A major project had scope creep or change order confusion that cost them money
- Their office manager or project coordinator quit and they realized how dependent the business was on that one person
- They won a large project and realized their current processes can't scale to handle the additional complexity
- They saw a competitor using technology to deliver a better client experience (polished client portal, automated updates, fast proposals)
- They hit a revenue milestone and want to grow further but know their back-office can't handle more volume

## Where to Find Them

### Online Channels
- **LinkedIn:** Search by title (owner, president, project manager, office manager) and company keywords (custom home builder, remodeling, HVAC). Company size filter: 2-50 employees.
- **Houzz Pro directory:** Custom builders and remodelers list their businesses here. Profiles show project photos, team size indicators, and service areas.
- **NAHB (National Association of Home Builders) member directory:** Filterable by specialty (custom builders, remodelers) and location.
- **NARI (National Association of the Remodeling Industry) member directory:** Focused specifically on remodelers.
- **NKBA (National Kitchen & Bath Association) directory:** Kitchen and bath remodelers specifically.
- **Google Business Profiles:** Search "[city] custom home builder" or "[city] kitchen remodeling." The profiles show reviews, photos, and sometimes team size.
- **Facebook Groups:** Local contractor groups, industry-specific groups (custom home builders, remodeling business owners). These are active communities where owners share challenges openly.
- **Instagram / Houzz:** High-end remodelers and custom builders showcase projects here. Active accounts suggest a business that cares about marketing but may not have automated operations.

### In-Person and Community
- NAHB International Builders' Show (annual, largest in the industry)
- Local HBA (Home Builders Association) chapter meetings
- NARI chapter events and networking
- Kitchen & Bath Industry Show (KBIS)
- Local contractor and trade association meetups
- Supplier and distributor events (lumber yards, appliance showrooms often host contractor events)

### Signals That a Company Is In-Market
- Job posting for an office manager, project coordinator, or admin (they're feeling the operational pain)
- Rapidly growing review count on Google or Houzz (more projects = more operational strain)
- Multiple active projects visible on their website or social media (capacity strain)
- LinkedIn posts from the owner about being busy or overwhelmed
- Recent award or recognition (NARI CotY, Houzz Best of) suggesting growth and reputation that their back-office may not be keeping up with
- They're using basic tools (spreadsheets, generic email) while competitors are using project management platforms (suggests they know they're behind but haven't invested yet)

## Disqualification Criteria

Do not pursue prospects who match any of the following:

1. **Revenue under $500K.** The project volume likely doesn't justify the investment. The ROI math won't be compelling enough.
2. **No repeatable workflows.** If every project is truly unique with no common process patterns, automation won't stick. (Rare in construction; most have standardized phases.)
3. **Tech-forward with internal IT capability.** If they have a dedicated operations or technology person who is already implementing automation, they're not our buyer. They'll try to build it themselves.
4. **General handyman or maintenance-only businesses.** Low-ticket, high-volume service calls are a different business model than high-ticket project work. The workflow complexity isn't there.
5. **Very large contractors (50+ employees or $20M+ revenue).** They likely have internal operations teams, formal IT departments, or are already evaluating enterprise solutions. Not our market.
6. **No decision-maker access.** If we can't reach the owner (at small companies) or the office manager/ops lead (at larger ones), the deal stalls. Don't invest time in companies where the contact can't make or influence the decision.
7. **Agencies or consulting firms.** They are competitors, not prospects. This includes marketing agencies, dev shops, and AI consultancies.
8. **Expecting a magic button.** If they think AI means zero human involvement from day one, they'll be disappointed. The agents handle the repeatable work, but a human still reviews and approves key decisions.
